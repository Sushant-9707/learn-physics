    Linear Momentum

    Momentum describes how difficult it is to stop a moving object. It combines mass and velocity into a single quantity that plays a central role in collisions and interactions between bodies.

    Core Ideas

    Definition of Momentum

    Linear momentum is defined as the product of an object's mass and velocity. It is a vector quantity, meaning it has both magnitude and direction.

    Objects with large mass or high velocity have greater momentum and are harder to stop or redirect.

    Impulse

    Impulse measures the effect of a force acting over a period of time. It is equal to the change in momentum of an object.

    This explains why extending the time of impact, such as using airbags, reduces the force experienced during collisions.

    Conservation of Momentum

    In an isolated system with no external forces, the total momentum remains constant. Momentum can be transferred between objects, but the total does not change.

    This principle governs everything from billiard ball collisions to rocket propulsion.

    Types of Collisions

    Collisions can be elastic or inelastic depending on whether kinetic energy is conserved.

    Momentum is conserved in all collisions, but kinetic energy is only conserved in perfectly elastic ones.
