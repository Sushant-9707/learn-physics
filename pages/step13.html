    Gravitation

    Gravitation describes the attraction between masses. At this stage, gravity is treated as a force acting at a distance. This model works extremely well for most everyday and planetary situations. However, this is not the complete picture. The deeper nature of gravity will be revisited later.

    Core Ideas

    Universal Law of Gravitation

    Every mass attracts every other mass with a force proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.

    This law explains phenomena ranging from falling objects to the motion of planets.

    Gravitational Field

    A gravitational field represents the influence a mass exerts on the space around it.

    It allows gravitational effects to be described without requiring direct contact between objects.

    Acceleration Due to Gravity

    Near the surface of Earth, all objects experience nearly the same gravitational acceleration, regardless of mass.

    This explains why heavy and light objects fall together when air resistance is negligible.

    Variation of g

    The value of gravitational acceleration changes with altitude, depth, and latitude.

    These variations arise due to Earth's shape, rotation, and distance from its center.

    Orbital Motion

    Orbital motion occurs when gravitational attraction provides the necessary centripetal force for circular or elliptical motion.

    Satellites remain in orbit by continuously falling around Earth.

    Is Gravity Really a Force?

    In classical physics, gravity is treated as a force. This approximation is extremely successful and practical.

    However, modern physics describes gravity not as a force, but as a curvature of spacetime caused by mass and energy. This deeper interpretation will be explored later.
