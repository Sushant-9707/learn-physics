    Magnetic Fields

    Magnetic fields describe how moving charges interact with space. Unlike electric fields, magnetism only appears when charges are in motion. This topic reveals that electricity and magnetism are deeply connected.

    Core Ideas

    Magnetic Field Concept

    A magnetic field is a region of space where a moving electric charge experiences a force. Unlike electric fields, magnetic fields do not act on stationary charges.

    This immediately tells us that magnetism is a relativistic effect of moving charges, not a separate fundamental interaction.

    Lorentz Force

    The Lorentz force describes how a charged particle behaves in electric and magnetic fields. The magnetic part of this force is always perpendicular to the velocity of the charge.

    Because of this perpendicular nature, magnetic fields change direction of motion, not the speed or kinetic energy of the particle.

    Motion of Charged Particles

    Charged particles moving in a uniform magnetic field follow circular or helical paths. The magnetic force acts as a centripetal force.

    This principle is used in cyclotrons, mass spectrometers, and particle accelerators.

    Force on Current-Carrying Wire

    A current-carrying conductor placed in a magnetic field experiences a force. This force arises from the combined magnetic forces on moving charges inside the wire.

    This principle is the foundation of electric motors.

    Biot–Savart Law

    The Biot–Savart law calculates the magnetic field produced by a small current element.

    It shows how magnetic fields depend on current magnitude, direction, and distance.

    Ampere’s Circuital Law

    Ampere’s law relates magnetic fields around a closed loop to the current passing through it.

    It simplifies magnetic field calculations for symmetric current distributions.

    Magnetic Materials

    Materials respond differently to magnetic fields depending on atomic structure. Diamagnetic, paramagnetic, and ferromagnetic materials behave in distinct ways.

    Ferromagnetism arises from aligned electron spins, not classical currents.
