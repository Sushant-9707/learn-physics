    Electromagnetic Induction

    Electromagnetic induction explains how changing magnetic fields generate electric currents. This phenomenon powers generators, transformers, and modern electrical infrastructure.

    Core Ideas

    Faraday’s Experiments

    Faraday discovered that electric current can be produced without batteries, simply by changing magnetic conditions.

    His experiments showed that motion or variation, not static fields, creates current.

    Magnetic Flux

    Magnetic flux measures how much magnetic field passes through a given surface.

    It depends on field strength, area, and orientation of the surface.

    Faraday’s Law

    Faraday’s law states that induced EMF is proportional to the rate of change of magnetic flux.

    Faster changes produce stronger induced currents.

    Lenz’s Law

    Lenz’s law gives the direction of induced current. The induced current always opposes the change that caused it.

    This law enforces conservation of energy.

    Motional EMF

    Motional EMF arises when a conductor moves through a magnetic field.

    Charges experience magnetic forces, leading to potential difference.

    Self Induction

    A changing current in a coil induces an EMF in the same coil.

    This effect resists sudden changes in current.

    Mutual Induction

    Mutual induction occurs when a changing current in one coil induces EMF in another.

    Transformers operate based on this principle.
