    Newton’s Laws of Motion

    Newton’s laws form the foundation of classical mechanics. They describe how forces affect the motion of objects and explain why objects move the way they do.

    Core Ideas

    First Law of Motion (Law of Inertia)

    An object remains at rest or continues to move with constant velocity unless acted upon by an external unbalanced force.

    This law introduces the concept of inertia, the resistance of an object to changes in its state of motion.

    Second Law of Motion

    The rate of change of momentum of an object is directly proportional to the applied force and occurs in the direction of the force.

    For constant mass, this law is commonly written as F = ma, linking force, mass, and acceleration.

    Third Law of Motion

    For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

    These action–reaction force pairs act on different objects and are always equal in magnitude and opposite in direction.

    Inertial Frames of Reference

    An inertial frame of reference is one in which Newton’s laws hold true without modification.

    Frames moving with constant velocity relative to an inertial frame are also inertial frames.
