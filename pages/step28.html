    Wave Optics

    Wave optics studies light as a wave phenomenon rather than a collection of rays. It explains effects that geometrical optics cannot, such as interference, diffraction, and polarization. These phenomena reveal that light spreads, overlaps, and interacts with itself in ways only waves can.

    Core Ideas

    Limits of Geometrical Optics

    Geometrical optics treats light as straight-line rays and works well when objects are much larger than the wavelength of light. However, it completely fails to explain why light bends around edges or produces patterns when passing through narrow openings.

    These failures force us to abandon the ray model and accept that light behaves as a wave under many circumstances.

    Huygens’ Principle

    Huygens’ principle states that every point on a wavefront acts as a source of secondary wavelets, and the new wavefront is the envelope of these wavelets.

    This idea provides a powerful way to understand reflection, refraction, and diffraction without relying on ray assumptions.

    Interference of Light

    Interference occurs when two or more light waves overlap and combine according to the principle of superposition. Depending on their relative phase, the waves can reinforce or cancel each other.

    This leads to alternating bright and dark regions, proving that light has a wave nature.

    Young’s Double Slit Experiment

    Young’s experiment demonstrated interference by passing light through two closely spaced slits. The resulting pattern of bright and dark fringes cannot be explained by particle or ray models.

    This experiment is one of the strongest classical proofs that light behaves as a wave.

    Diffraction of Light

    Diffraction is the bending and spreading of light when it passes through a narrow opening or around an obstacle. It becomes significant when the size of the opening is comparable to the wavelength of light.

    Diffraction shows that light does not travel strictly in straight lines.

    Single Slit Diffraction

    In single slit diffraction, light spreads out after passing through a narrow slit, producing a central bright maximum surrounded by weaker fringes. The pattern arises from interference between wavelets across the slit.

    This phenomenon sets a fundamental limit on optical resolution.

    Polarization

    Polarization refers to restricting the direction of oscillation of the electric field in a light wave. Only transverse waves can be polarized.

    The existence of polarization confirms that light is a transverse wave.

    Coherence of Light

    Coherence describes the ability of light waves to produce stable interference patterns. For sustained interference, the waves must maintain a constant phase difference.

    Lasers are highly coherent sources, which is why they are central to wave optics experiments.
