    Kinetic Theory of Gases

    The kinetic theory of gases explains macroscopic properties like pressure and temperature by analyzing the microscopic motion of gas molecules. It connects everyday observations with molecular-level physics.

    Core Ideas

    Assumptions of Kinetic Theory

    The kinetic theory assumes that gases consist of a very large number of tiny particles moving randomly in all directions. These particles are treated as point masses with negligible volume compared to the space between them.

    Collisions between molecules and container walls are assumed to be perfectly elastic, meaning no energy is lost during collisions.

    Origin of Pressure

    Gas pressure arises due to repeated collisions of gas molecules with the walls of a container. Each collision transfers momentum to the wall, producing a force.

    The collective effect of billions of such collisions per second results in measurable pressure.

    Meaning of Temperature

    Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of gas molecules. Higher temperature means molecules move faster on average.

    It does not depend on the size of the gas sample but on molecular motion.

    RMS Speed of Molecules

    Root Mean Square (RMS) speed represents the effective average speed of gas molecules. It is directly related to temperature and inversely related to molecular mass.

    Lighter molecules move faster than heavier ones at the same temperature.

    Degrees of Freedom

    Degrees of freedom refer to the independent ways in which a molecule can store energy, such as translational, rotational, and vibrational motion.

    The number depends on the molecular structure of the gas.

    Law of Equipartition of Energy

    This law states that energy is equally distributed among all available degrees of freedom of a system in thermal equilibrium.

    It helps predict internal energy and heat capacity of gases.

    Limitations of the Theory

    The kinetic theory works well for ideal gases but fails at high pressures or low temperatures.

    Intermolecular forces and molecular size become significant in real gases.
